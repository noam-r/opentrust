# 7. Exceptions, Enforcement, and Failure

## Why this section matters

Many governance proposals sound strong until they encounter a hard case, a breach, or institutional fatigue. A serious model must therefore explain not only how ordinary life works, but also how the institution handles stress.

## Exceptions

A humane community fund cannot pretend that every deserving case will fit neatly inside standard criteria. Exceptional cases are inevitable.

The right response is neither:

- to ban exceptions entirely
- nor to allow them casually

Instead, exceptions should be treated as a special governance category.

### Principles for exceptions

Exceptions should be:

- explicitly named as exceptions
- justified against a declared standard
- approved by higher or different authority than ordinary cases
- logged in a way that remains visible to audit and oversight
- prevented from silently becoming the new norm

The purpose of this is not to eliminate mercy. It is to ensure that mercy does not become a cover for arbitrary power.

## Breach and enforcement

A contract without consequences is only aspirational. Therefore the conceptual model must include some answer to the question:

> What happens when a body or officer acts outside authority?

Possible categories of breach include:

- acting outside delegated power
- misclassifying an exceptional case as ordinary
- hiding a conflict of interest
- providing false or incomplete reporting
- bypassing required process
- misusing private data
- failing to maintain required institutional roles or controls

At the theoretical level, the model should recognize that breaches require a path of response. This may include review, reversal, suspension of authority, escalation, public disclosure of breach type, or removal from office, depending on the severity and governance form chosen.

## Accountability is not only audit

Audit identifies and reviews. Accountability requires that findings can lead to consequence.

If a system can detect breaches but cannot correct them, then it remains vulnerable to ritualized transparency with little practical force.

## Institutional failure and continuity

A serious communal institution must also have a theory of failure.

The fund should be able to answer questions such as:

- what happens if a required body becomes inactive?
- what if quorum cannot be reached?
- what if validators resign and cannot be replaced?
- what if repayment collapses or the reserve becomes inadequate?
- what if governance is deadlocked?
- what if the fund can no longer operate within its own contract?

These questions are not technical edge cases. They are part of the institution's credibility.

## Pause, repair, and dissolution

The contract should therefore contemplate more than normal operation. It should include the conceptual possibility of:

- temporary pause
- emergency containment
- institutional repair
- orderly wind-down
- final dismantling

Dismantling rules matter especially because communal institutions often continue in name long after they cease to function well in reality.

## Why failure rules increase trust

Paradoxically, an institution becomes more trustworthy when it admits the possibility of its own failure and defines what happens then.

A fund that says "we have rules for breach, deadlock, and shutdown" appears more credible than one that assumes its own moral continuity forever.

## The broader principle

This entire section rests on a simple idea:

> Institutions should not only define how power is used when things go well. They should define how power is contained when things go badly.

That principle is essential to the integrity of the model.
