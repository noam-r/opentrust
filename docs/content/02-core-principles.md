# 2. Core Principles

This proposal rests on a small number of principles. These principles matter more than any particular mechanism, because they define the spirit of the institutional model.

## 1. Privacy should protect vulnerability, not power

A borrower may need privacy for the sake of dignity, safety, or ethical reasons. A donor may sometimes require discretion, including forms of discreet giving. These are legitimate goods.

But privacy must not become a shield for the unreviewable exercise of authority.

> The system should hide vulnerable people where necessary, not hide the conduct of those who govern communal money.

This is the foundational distinction.

## 2. Trust should be structured, not assumed

The goal is not to create a trustless system. Communal finance will always require human judgment and institutional trust.

The goal is to transform trust from something vague and personality-based into something:

- bounded
- role-specific
- distributed
- reviewable
- auditable

In this model, people are trusted for defined tasks under declared limits, not because their personal reputation is expected to substitute for institutional design.

## 3. The fund should be governed by public rules, not private understandings

A community lending fund should not depend primarily on informal custom or personal authority. Its legitimacy should come from a declared framework that defines:

- what the fund exists to do
- what it may and may not fund
- who may decide what
- how exceptions are handled
- how the rules themselves can be changed

This proposal treats governance rules as constitutive, not optional.

## 4. The most sensitive information should be seen by the narrowest function

Not everyone involved in the life of the fund should see the same information.

Where the information is most private, the mandate should be most limited.

This leads to a core principle:

> The people with access to the most sensitive details should have the narrowest discretionary authority.

That is why factual validation and allocative judgment should be separated.

## 5. Discretion may exist, but only within declared bounds

This framework does not assume that all decisions can be fully automated or reduced to formulas.

A humane institution needs some discretion. But discretion should never be:

- undefined
- hidden
- unlimited
- immune from review

Discretion is acceptable only when it is bounded by rule, assigned to a legitimate body, and visible as discretion.

## 6. Exceptions are legitimate, but dangerous

Real communal life always produces cases that do not fit perfectly into standard criteria. A viable community fund must have some capacity to handle exceptional need.

But every exception creates a governance risk. If exceptions become common, informal, or invisible, the contract becomes ceremonial.

Therefore:

- exceptions should be explicitly named as exceptions
- exceptions should require elevated authority
- exceptions should be documented and auditable
- exceptions should not silently redefine the ordinary rules

## 7. Accountability must be meaningful, not decorative

Publishing aggregate numbers is not enough. A system can look transparent while leaving its real power dynamics untouched.

Meaningful accountability requires that people can know not only what happened, but also:

- what was allowed to happen
- who had authority to act
- whether procedures were followed
- where an exception occurred
- whether a breach can be reviewed and corrected

## 8. Governance must preserve dignity as well as integrity

This proposal is not anti-solidarity and not anti-flexibility. It does not aim to replace compassion with bureaucracy for its own sake.

Its ambition is more precise:

> to make a community fund more governable without making it less humane.

Any version of the model that destroys dignity, humiliates borrowers, or turns urgent need into procedural cruelty would betray its own purpose.

## Summary principle

The entire framework can be condensed into one sentence:

> A community fund should protect the privacy of vulnerable people while making the exercise of financial power rule-bound, reviewable, and institutionally limited.
