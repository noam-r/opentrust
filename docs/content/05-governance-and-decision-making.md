# 5. Governance and Decision-Making

## The core problem of discretion

In many community funds, the deepest governance problem is not bookkeeping but discretion. Someone, somewhere, decides:

- who is eligible
- who is urgent
- which cases are ordinary
- which exceptions are acceptable
- when repayment pressure is applied or relaxed
- when a "special case" overrides the usual pattern

If that discretion is concentrated and weakly bounded, the institution remains vulnerable even with decent records.

## From personal control to bounded governance

This framework proposes a move away from personal control and toward bounded governance.

The question is not simply, "Who is trustworthy enough to decide?" The better question is:

> **Which kinds of decisions should belong to which bodies, under what limits, and with what review?**

That is the heart of the governance model.

## Rule-bound decisions, delegated decisions, and collective decisions

A mature fund should distinguish among at least three categories of decisions.

### 1. Rule-bound ordinary decisions

These are cases that clearly fit the contract and remain within normal size, category, and exposure limits. They may be handled through ordinary delegated processes.

### 2. Delegated discretionary decisions

These are cases that fit within a declared zone of discretion granted to a defined body. The key requirement is that the scope of this discretion is publicly bounded, not informal.

### 3. Collective or escalated decisions

These are cases that, due to amount, risk, exceptional nature, or cumulative exposure, require broader approval by the relevant governing body.

This layered model avoids two extremes:

- total centralization
- total democracy over every minor decision

## Thresholds

The threshold idea is one of the strongest elements of the model.

Not every modest request should require broad deliberation. That would be slow, invasive, and impractical. But major allocations, repeated exceptions, or large percentages of total fund exposure should not be left to a small operational group.

Thresholds therefore serve several purposes:

- preserve efficiency for routine cases
- reserve collective authority for material risk
- make escalation predictable
- reduce quiet overreach by administrators

Thresholds should be understood not only in monetary terms, but potentially also in terms of:

- share of total fund
- cumulative exposure to a borrower class
- exception frequency
- repayment risk
- mission sensitivity

## Democracy: useful, but not enough

A broader voting process can reduce the power of a single manager or small clique. That is a real advantage.

But democracy by itself is not automatically fairness. A group can still be biased, pressured, cliquish, or poorly informed. For that reason, voting must operate within a contract. Otherwise the institution merely moves from private discretion of one person to private discretion of many people.

## The role of the contract in governance

The contract should define:

- what decisions are automatic or rule-governed
- what decisions may be delegated
- what decisions require vote
- what counts as a quorum
- whether a simple or special majority is needed
- which actions are forbidden even by majority
- how exceptions are surfaced
- who may propose amendments

This ensures that governance is not only participatory, but also constitutional.

## Meaningful but privacy-preserving participation

A governance body should not vote blindly, but neither should it need unrestricted access to personal files.

The ideal is to allow meaningful allocative judgment on the basis of anonymized but relevant information, such as:

- amount requested
- category of need
- contract fit
- repayment terms or risk classification
- whether the case is ordinary or exceptional
- exposure impact on the fund

That allows members to govern without turning privacy into committee spectacle.

## Conflict of interest

A governance model that ignores conflicts of interest is not serious. In communal settings, pressure often comes through relationships rather than outright theft.

Therefore the conceptual model should explicitly require that:

- decision-makers disclose relevant conflicts
- conflicted participants recuse themselves where appropriate
- repeated related-party patterns remain visible for audit and review

## Governance as legitimacy

The purpose of governance in this model is not to make the process feel participatory for its own sake. It is to create legitimate, bounded, and reviewable authority.

A good governance system should let outsiders say:

- the rules of power are known
- not all authority is concentrated in one hand
- members are not merely symbolic
- major deviations cannot occur quietly

That is the standard the model should aim to meet.
