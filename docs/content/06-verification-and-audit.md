# 6. Verification and Audit

## Why verification must be separated from judgment

One of the major risks in a privacy-preserving model is that the people who see sensitive documents become the real hidden decision-makers.

To avoid this, the proposal introduces a distinct verification function.

Its purpose is not to decide who deserves help. Its purpose is to determine whether the factual criteria defined by the contract have been met.

## Delegation of trust

This can be understood as a controlled delegation of trust.

The fund may empower a defined validating body, consisting of multiple people rather than a single manager, to review sensitive identity and supporting documentation. The broader governance body does not need to see those private materials directly. Instead, it relies on an auditable attestation that the relevant criteria have or have not been satisfied.

This preserves privacy while reducing the need for blind personal trust.

## The validation body's proper role

The validating body should be bureaucratic in character, not moralizing in character. It should operate as a fact-validation layer.

Its role may include questions such as:

- was identity verified?
- were required documents submitted?
- were the documents recent and complete enough under the rule set?
- does the evidence place the applicant inside or outside the relevant eligibility category?
- is there a declared red flag?
- is the case ordinary, borderline, or exceptional under the contract?

Its role should not be to answer:

- is this person worthy?
- do we personally trust this family?
- are they the kind of people we like to help?
- should we bend the rules because the story is moving?

That distinction is central.

## Why this helps

Separating validation from allocative judgment creates several gains:

- fewer people see humiliating or highly sensitive details
- no single person monopolizes knowledge
- the broader governance body does not vote in factual darkness
- favoritism is harder to disguise as private knowledge
- later audit is more feasible because validation outputs can be reviewed against stated criteria

## Auditability

This model becomes especially strong when the outputs of validation are structured and auditable.

The more the system can express validation as clear determinations tied to declared criteria, the easier it becomes for an audit function to review questions such as:

- was the required evidence actually present?
- was the case classified correctly?
- was an exception mislabeled as ordinary compliance?
- did validators act outside their mandate?
- were edge cases treated consistently?

This does not mean validation is always perfectly mechanical. Borderline cases will still exist. But the aim is to make interpretation visible rather than hidden inside informal discretion.

## The audit function

Audit should be treated as a distinct oversight role rather than as a symbolic afterthought.

At a conceptual level, the audit function should be able to review:

- procedural compliance
- contract compliance
- financial consistency
- conflict-of-interest handling
- exception frequency and treatment
- validator conduct
- administrative conduct
- completeness and honesty of reporting

The key point is that no body in the system should become effectively unreviewable.

## The risk of verifier capture

The validating layer is valuable, but it creates its own risk: a new hidden center of power.

That is why the validating body should itself be governed by clear rules such as:

- plural composition
- bounded mandate
- recusal rules
- auditability
- documented actions
- term limits or rotation where appropriate

In other words, validators are not "the trusted people." They are holders of a narrow institutional office.

## Why this pillar matters

The validation-and-audit structure is one of the main ways this proposal seeks to reconcile dignity and accountability.

It allows the system to say:

- private facts are not exposed more widely than needed
- those facts are still not controlled by a single person
- compliance is attested in a form that can later be reviewed
- governance can proceed on the basis of verified, privacy-preserving information

This is one of the strongest conceptual pillars of the entire model.
