# 3. The Institutional Model

## The proposal in one sentence

A community lending fund should be understood as a **contract-governed communal fund** rather than as a pool of money managed primarily through personal trust.

This paper refers to that model as **OpenTrust**.

## Why a contract matters

The contract is not a secondary policy document. It is the constitutional foundation of the fund. It defines the institution itself.

Without a constituting contract, governance tends to drift toward:

- informal custom
- personality-based control
- unclear authority
- ad hoc exceptions
- weak grounds for review

With a contract, the fund can be understood as acting under declared legitimacy rather than inherited habit.

## What the contract should define

At a theoretical level, the contract should define at least:

- the purpose of the fund
- who the intended beneficiaries are
- what kinds of loans or aid are in scope
- what uses of money are forbidden
- governance structure
- authority boundaries
- reporting and audit obligations
- conflict-of-interest rules
- amendment procedures
- continuity and dismantling rules

The contract should not merely describe the fund's ideals. It should define its permissible action space.

## The institutional bodies

The model assumes that authority is divided across distinct functions. Exact implementation can vary, but conceptually the fund should recognize different bodies such as:

### Administrative body

Handles operations, recordkeeping, communications, and routine fund mechanics. This body should not own the entire decision process simply because it handles day-to-day work.

### Validation body

Reviews sensitive identity and supporting documentation in order to determine whether factual criteria have been met. This body should validate facts, not decide who "deserves" aid.

### Governing or voting body

Makes allocative decisions within the framework of the contract, based on meaningful but privacy-preserving information. This body should not need unrestricted access to personal files.

### Audit or oversight body

Reviews whether the institution acted according to contract, procedure, and truth. This body exists to ensure that no part of the system becomes practically immune from review.

These bodies may interact, but they should not collapse into one another without explicit reason.

## Separation of powers

The deeper logic of the model is institutional separation:

- those who **know the most private facts** should not automatically control all outcomes
- those who **allocate money** should not be free to invent facts or bypass verification
- those who **audit** should not depend on the goodwill of the people they review

This separation reduces the amount of power held by any single office.

## Legitimacy

The fund's legitimacy should come from a combination of:

- consent to the contract
- adherence to declared rules
- bounded delegation of authority
- visible accountability mechanisms

At this stage, different governance theories remain possible. For example, legitimacy might be grounded mainly in donor consent, in member governance, in trustee stewardship, or in some hybrid. That remains an open question for expert discussion.

## Not all participants are the same

The model should distinguish among different actors, because conflating them creates confusion. At minimum, the theory should recognize separate roles for:

- donors
- borrowers or applicants
- members with governance rights
- administrators
- validators
- auditors
- contract custodians or trustees, if such a role exists

This matters because the answer to "who gets to decide?" depends on what kind of actor they are.

## Time, continuity, and institutional life cycle

A serious institution must also define its own life cycle. The contract should therefore address conceptual questions such as:

- how bodies are selected
- maximum terms of office
- replacement mechanisms
- minimum operating conditions
- inactivity or deadlock procedures
- orderly pause or shutdown
- rules for dismantling the fund and distributing remaining assets

This prevents the institution from becoming immortal in theory but ungoverned in practice.

## The key institutional shift

The crucial shift can be stated simply:

> The community fund is no longer treated as a trust-me operation run by known people. It is treated as a governed institution whose powers, procedures, and limits are defined in advance.

That is the heart of the model.
