# OpenTrust

## Purpose of this paper

This site presents **OpenTrust**, a theoretical framework for a new kind of community lending fund: a **contract-governed communal fund** designed to improve transparency, reduce corruption, preserve dignity, and distribute trust more responsibly.

This is **not** a design document and **not** a technical specification. Its purpose is to explain the idea clearly enough to invite serious feedback from experts in communal governance, finance, law, halacha, philanthropy, and institutional design.

## The central claim

A community lending fund should not have to choose between two bad models:

- **opaque trust**, where the public is asked to rely on the word of a small inner circle
- **full exposure**, where dignity and privacy are damaged in the name of accountability

The claim of this paper is that there is a third model:

> A community fund can protect the privacy of vulnerable people while making the exercise of financial power rule-bound, reviewable, and institutionally limited.

In this model, trust is not eliminated. It is **structured**.

## The core idea in one paragraph

The fund begins with a public contract that defines its purpose, eligibility logic, governance structure, authority boundaries, reporting duties, exception rules, amendment rules, and dismantling procedures. Authority is split across distinct bodies such as administration, validation, and audit. Sensitive borrower identity and documents are exposed only to a narrow, auditable verification function. Broader governance can review and approve requests through meaningful but privacy-preserving case summaries. The goal is to replace personality-based trust with a system in which privacy protects the vulnerable, while transparency constrains power.

## Why this matters

Many community lending funds are built on goodwill and mutual aid, but also on concentrated discretion and low visibility. That can make them compassionate and agile, but also structurally vulnerable to:

- favoritism
- hidden conflicts of interest
- misuse of funds
- mission drift
- non-transparent exceptions
- erosion of donor confidence
- corruption that is hard to detect or prove

This framework asks whether a community fund can remain deeply humane while also becoming more governable, auditable, and trustworthy.

## What makes this proposal different

The novelty here is not simply digitizing bookkeeping or adding a dashboard. The novelty is conceptual:

- the fund is constituted by a **contract**, not informal understandings
- power is divided across **defined organs**, not concentrated in one person
- privacy and transparency are treated as **separate design questions**
- factual verification is separated from allocative judgment
- exceptions are recognized as legitimate but bounded
- trust is treated as something to be **distributed, limited, and audited**

## What this paper covers

This paper is organized around eight questions:

1. What problem is this model trying to solve?
2. What are its guiding principles?
3. What kind of institution is being proposed?
4. How can privacy and transparency coexist?
5. How should decisions be made?
6. What role do validation and audit play?
7. How should exceptions, breaches, and institutional failure be handled?
8. Why would people adopt such a model, and what tradeoffs does it involve?

## Intended audience

This paper is meant for people who can critically evaluate the idea before any technical implementation begins, including:

- community fund operators
- communal leaders
- philanthropists and donors
- governance experts
- legal and compliance professionals
- ethical and ethical advisors
- institutional designers

## Working assumption

The proposal is deliberately framed at a theoretical level. Some points will remain open. That is intentional. The goal at this stage is not to pretend the model is finished, but to make it precise enough that experts can meaningfully challenge, improve, or reject it.
