# 8. Business Model

## Purpose

OpenTrust is envisioned as a **commercial product for nonprofits, charitable organizations, and community funds**. It is not itself the charitable fund. Rather, it is the infrastructure that helps such funds operate with stronger governance, clearer reporting, and more credible administration.

This distinction matters. The fund remains mission-driven and zero-interest; OpenTrust provides the operating system around it.

## Core principle

A zero-interest lending fund is not a zero-cost institution.

Even when no interest is charged, real costs still exist, including:

- payment processing fees
- bank transfer costs
- reconciliation work
- validation and governance workflows
- reporting and audit infrastructure
- administrative and operational handling

The business model for OpenTrust should therefore make those costs visible, structured, and commercially sustainable without confusing them with the charitable purpose of the fund itself.

## Commercial framing

OpenTrust should be framed as:

> a governance and operations platform for community lending funds

Its customers are organizations, not borrowers. The platform is used by nonprofits and charitable institutions that want to run funds in a more transparent, privacy-preserving, and accountable way.

This means OpenTrust does not earn money by charging interest to people in need. It earns money by selling infrastructure to institutions.

## Two commercial modes

The business model likely needs to support two different modes of adoption.

### 1. Full-package mode

In this mode, OpenTrust provides both the governance platform and the money-movement layer.

This may include:

- donation intake
- payment processing
- disbursement flows
- reconciliation support
- governance workflows
- validation workflows
- reporting and audit structure

In this model, OpenTrust can charge a **bundled transaction-based operating fee**.

For example, if underlying card processing costs are approximately 1.5%, OpenTrust may charge an all-in rate such as 2.0% or 2.5%, where the spread covers:

- payment infrastructure
- operational handling
- risk
- platform administration
- commercial margin

This can also be priced in tiers, such as:

- up to a certain monthly volume: one rate
- higher monthly volumes: lower rates
- enterprise scale: custom pricing

The value of this model is simplicity. Organizations can adopt OpenTrust without a separate software budget line, because the operating cost is embedded in the transaction rail.

### 2. Core-only mode

Some organizations will already have payment infrastructure, banking relationships, or internal finance systems.

In those cases, OpenTrust should still be usable as a governance and workflow layer without requiring the organization to adopt OpenTrust's money-movement rail.

In this mode, OpenTrust provides:

- contract-governed fund management
- role and body structure
- validation workflows
- governance and voting workflows
- exception handling
- audit trails
- reporting and transparency controls

Because OpenTrust is not handling payment processing in this mode, it cannot rely on transaction margin alone. It therefore needs a separate pricing model, such as:

- annual subscription
- per-fund license
- tiered organizational pricing
- enterprise pricing
- setup and onboarding fees

## Why the model should be hybrid

A hybrid model is likely necessary because the market is heterogeneous.

Some organizations want a turnkey operating system. Others already have mature payment infrastructure and only need the governance, validation, and transparency layer.

The hybrid model allows OpenTrust to say:

- use OpenTrust as a full-stack fund operating platform
- or use OpenTrust as a governance layer on top of your existing payment rails

This makes adoption easier across different institutional sizes and levels of maturity.

## Important pricing principles

Whatever specific pricing model is chosen, several principles should remain constant.

### Pricing must be explicit

Organizations should know what they are paying for. OpenTrust does not need to disclose its commercial agreements with processors, but it should clearly declare its own rates.

For example:

- card processing through OpenTrust: 2.0%
- wire or bank-transfer handling: defined fee schedule
- core-only platform license: defined annual or monthly pricing

The key is clarity.

### Bundled pricing should not be described as free

If the full-package model embeds cost inside the transaction rail, that may reduce budget friction, but it is still a real cost.

Therefore the honest framing is not:

- "it doesn't cost money"

but rather:

- "it does not require a separate software budget line"

That distinction protects trust.

### Borrowers should not be the payer

As a rule, OpenTrust should avoid charging borrower-facing fees that resemble hidden interest or tolls on hardship.

Its commercial relationship should be with the organization operating the fund, even if that cost is recovered through the fund's disclosed operating structure.

### Platform revenue should remain distinguishable from unavoidable third-party costs

Even if OpenTrust offers an all-in rate, conceptually it is still useful to distinguish between:

- unavoidable transaction costs
- OpenTrust's operating and commercial margin

This does not require public disclosure of processor contracts, but it does matter internally and ethically.

## Strategic strengths of this model

This business model has several strengths.

### Lower adoption friction

Bundled transaction pricing can be easier to adopt than a standalone software subscription, especially for smaller organizations.

### Better alignment with real operations

Organizations already expect payment movement to cost money. Embedding OpenTrust into that operational layer makes the product feel native to the work rather than added on top of it.

### Flexibility across customer types

The hybrid structure allows OpenTrust to serve both:

- smaller organizations that want a complete operating system
- larger organizations that want only the governance and control layer

### More durable value proposition

The model recognizes that OpenTrust's value lies not only in moving money, but also in governing it. That makes the product more defensible over time.

## Main risks and cautions

This model also has risks.

### Perception of monetizing donations

If bundled transaction pricing is not explained carefully, critics may frame it as taking money out of charitable flows. This is why the pricing must be explicit and the value provided must be concrete.

### Confusion between platform fee and processor fee

If the all-in rate is presented carelessly, organizations may misunderstand what portion reflects external cost and what portion reflects OpenTrust's revenue. Clear commercial language helps prevent this.

### Resistance from sophisticated organizations

Larger institutions may prefer unbundled pricing or may already have optimized payment infrastructure. This is one reason the core-only mode is important.

## Working conclusion

The most likely business model for OpenTrust is a **hybrid commercial model**:

- a **full-package mode** with bundled transaction-based pricing for organizations that want OpenTrust to handle money movement and operations
- a **core-only mode** with direct platform pricing for organizations that want to keep their existing payment infrastructure

This preserves the charitable nature of the fund while allowing OpenTrust to operate as a sustainable commercial product.

## One-sentence summary

OpenTrust should be commercial infrastructure for charitable lending funds: monetized either through bundled operating fees when it handles the payment rail, or through direct platform pricing when organizations use it only for governance, validation, and transparency.
