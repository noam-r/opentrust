# 4. Privacy and Transparency

## The governing tension

A community lending fund lives inside a real moral tension.

On one side, people may require privacy for reasons of dignity, safety, modesty, or ethical value. On the other side, opacity creates fertile ground for favoritism, misuse, and corruption.

A viable model cannot ignore either side.

## The central distinction

The key distinction is this:

> **privacy of persons is not the same as secrecy of institutions**

A borrower may remain hidden. A private donor may remain hidden. But the operation of authority, the movement of funds, the use of exceptions, and the observance of rules must not disappear behind a general privacy claim.

## What privacy should cover

At a theoretical level, privacy may legitimately cover matters such as:

- borrower identity
- personal hardship details
- private financial records
- medical or family documentation
- guarantor identity
- donor identity in cases of justified discretion
- any sensitive evidence that would expose or humiliate an applicant

These are the kinds of information the system should expose only to those whose role requires it.

## What privacy should not cover

Privacy should not be used to hide matters such as:

- aggregate inflows and outflows
- total outstanding obligations
- general fund exposure
- categories of use
- policy rules
- governance rules
- who had authority to act
- whether a decision was ordinary or exceptional
- conflicts of interest
- whether procedures were followed
- whether auditors or reviewers found breaches

This is where transparency has moral force.

## Visibility should be governed

Visibility should itself be treated as a governed question. Different categories of participants should have access to different layers of information.

At a conceptual level, the model should distinguish among information that is:

- public
- visible to donors
- visible to governance participants
- visible only to validators
- visible only to auditors or exceptional review bodies

The exact boundaries can later be specified, but the principle matters now: access is not casual. It is role-bound.

## Why full exposure is not the answer

A fully open committee model would reduce some kinds of hidden power, but it would also create serious problems:

- borrower humiliation
- excessive spread of sensitive information
- deterrence from applying
- committee gossip or social leakage
- moral injury to the ideal of dignified aid

The proposal therefore rejects full exposure as a simplistic answer.

## Why full secrecy is also unacceptable

At the same time, simply saying "this is private" cannot be enough.

If privacy is left undefined, it becomes a convenient language for hiding:

- managerial power
- repeated exceptions
- insider favoritism
- contract violations
- misuse of restricted funds
- selective reporting

Therefore privacy must be principled, narrow, and auditable.

## Donor confidence without voyeurism

One of the aims of this model is to let a donor trust the institution without requiring direct access to borrower identity.

A donor should be able to know, in substance:

- what the fund is permitted to do
- whether the fund acted within those limits
- what categories of needs were funded
- whether exceptional cases occurred
- whether validators and auditors confirmed compliance
- whether the fund remains solvent and rule-bound

This creates informed trust without voyeurism.

## A useful summary formula

The relationship between privacy and transparency can be summarized as follows:

- protect identities where dignity requires it
- reveal institutional behavior where accountability requires it
- limit access to the minimum needed for each role
- never allow a privacy claim to make authority unreviewable

That is the balance this framework seeks.
